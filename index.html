God and Justice: Religious Movements in the Civil Rights Era

Thesis:

Religion within America became a refuge for the African American community when faced with adversity. Churches are a place of not only religious congregation but also a place of social and political action. Particularly within the African American church religion has given a powerful platform so that religious beliefs were used to advocate for civil rights, and help bring identity to the African American community.

Address to the National Catholic Conference for Interracial Justice

The address to the National Catholic Conference for Interracial Justice shows the significant role religion played in the Civil Rights Movement. There were numerous leaders within the Catholic church who helped advocate for racial equality, framing the struggle against segregation and discrimination as a moral and spiritual issue. Mostly touching on the topic that there is dignity for every human person and used the church's teachings to provide a foundation for racial justice to help mobilize groups to engage in social action. This shows that religion played an active role when it came to social issues.

    Author's Involvement:

  • The author Diane Nash talks about her personal involvement within the Civil Rights Movement, dealing with sit-ins as a student.

  • Talked about how the movement was grounded with religious principles, and with the goal of building a community that is free from segregation and oppression.

    • Freedom Rides:

  • The Freedom Rides were important during the movement, where both white and black activists faced difficulties while trying to desegregate transportation.

  • Most participants believed that their participation in the movement was essential for their Christian commitment, and that it was God's will.

  • This was a direct response to the Rosa Parks' situation that we talked about in module 13. This shows how African Americans were tired with having to deal with the oppression they faced on a day-to-day.

    • Purpose:

  • During class we talked about how most religions are not just within the mind but also deals with people being pro-active in the physical world. It was shown within the source that active participation was one of the biggest ideas within the Christian faith.

Letter from Birmingham Jail

The letter from Birmingham Jail was written by Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. in 1963, and became one of the most important text during the Civil Rights Movement. This document was written to critize those who were not as involved in the civil rights movement. This document was used to justify King's strategy of nonviolent civil disobedience. King stated that the fight for civil rights was deeply rooted in Christian principles. He used biblical teachings of love, justice, and equality and by using religous context within the fight for rights it encourged others who followed Christianity to join the fight for the rights of others. This helped to gain suport from white moderates and other religious communites who became key pieces in the fight for racial equality.

    Religious Foundations:

  • Dr. Martin Luther King Jr.'s core arguements included Christian teachings that emphasized love, justice, and moral responsibility.

  • Stressed that nonviolent protest were the only way to reach true change. Also believed that nonviolence was both a moral and spiritual choice.

    • Purpose:

  • Used faith as the basis for those who were not already involved to become involved and show immediate action. Talked about how "waiting for the right time" was a contradiction to the Christian teachings people followed.

  • Compared the struggles that African Americans faced for justice to those that Christ experienced. Displaying that hardship can help lead to a greater good.

  • His commitment to his faith and nonviolent protests support what we have learned in class that sometimes people take action within their community and it is not just something internal. Their faith is practiced through actions and can be displayed by there profound voice in public spheres, and his active role in the Civil Rights Movement.

I Have a Dream speech by Martin Luther King

The I have a dream speech has religous themes reflecting King's faith and his moral standpoint when it comes to justice and equality. Throughout the speech, king uses biblical references, using God as a central figure in the fight for freedom. He talks about how everyone is equal under God's eyes. This talks about the Christian belief that there is dignity for every individual. At one point King talks about a promised land that gives similar contrast to the idea of israelites' freedom. Overall the speech helps reinforce the idea that the struggle for justice is the will of God.


    Rooted in Faith:

  • "We hold these truths to be self-evident, that all men are created equal", shows the Christian value that human equality must be achieved and that all people are made in the image of God.

  • The "I Have a Dream" speech can be seen as a display of religious practice due to its intersection of religious ideation and principles. King also references freedom and justice through the speech and for God's deliverance from these hardships.

  • This speech could also been seen as a prayer due to his use of the words "With this faith, we will be able to work together, to pray together, to struggle together... knowing that we will be free one day". Supports the idea that Martin Luther King is trying to push the idea of a Kingdom of God on Earth were everyone lives freely and equally.

How the Black Church saved Black America

This source talks about the role of the Black Church had shaping African American, political activism, culture, and resilience. He argued that the Black church was an instrument in perserving black culture and fostering change. From people like Frederick Douglass to Martin Luther King Jr., he talks about how the black church became a space that developed change and intellecutal forms that deeply contributed to the Civil Rights Movement. He describes the church as a "parent" of the movement. The church's ability to organize, commit to justice, and also create a foundation of moral authority was crucial. As the fight for justice was often just seen as an extension of their faith.


    Connection:

  • The Black Church was not just a physical building but rather a place where African Americans were able to come together, pray, and seek strength with one another.

  • The church also allowed for the ability to have a safe and secure place to congregate and organize social action.

  • The idea that the church was a "parent" of the Civil Rights Movement derives from the idea that it gave a space for the nurturing of intellectual, social, and political talk. This also allowed individuals to gain a sense of faith-based identity, giving strength in time of need.

Conclusion

Religion within America became a refuge for the African American community when faced with adversity. Church became a source of moral guidance but also a catalyst for social change. The Black Church became the foundation that leaders such as Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. used to advocate for racial equality and justice. Religion and the Civil Rights Movement were heavily intertwined by allowing those struggling to find strength but also allowing African Americans to have a space where they were allowed to organize. The idea of nonviolent civil disobedience that was derived from Christian teachings was also a driving force that propelled the Civil Rights Movement.

The Civil Rights Movement used the idea of faith as a moral framework to battle racial injustice. The idea that social change would only be achieved if individuals saw the dependence on God and followed the principles of love, justice, and equality. In the letter of the Birmingham Jail, King critiqued moderate white Christians who would not help with the movement. During class, we talked about how often religion is used as a means of practice. This argued that if you were not actively trying to combat segregation then you were not truly practicing your faith. Showing that the idea of religion is more of a belief of matter rather than a matter of belief. Talking about the idea of loving thy neighbor also helped bring white religious communities to join the fight for racial justice, transforming the movement into a “coalition of Christian faiths”.

Religious practices and symbols also were used during the civil rights movement. During the Selma to Montgomery march activists recited prayers and sang gospel hymns. The march for many participants was more than just a political protest but also an expression of their own faith. This march also helped with the fight toward the Voting Rights Act of 1965. The Black Church was more than just a place of worship during this time, it was a central part of the resistance. This allowed African Americans to build a community and strategize for change. Within the church those who previously did not have a voice found theirs and used it as a means for change. Using the theology that you believe in and then acting on it so that your environment is something that we talked about in class as well. The church provided a moral foundation for the movement by underlining justice, equality, and the dignity of the human. Those were where the conversation was first held before going out to the public and professing what they believed in.

Other leaders within religious communities were also crucial to the success. During the national catholic conference for interracial justice, Nash talked about how segregation and racism were a profound sin against human dignity and explained her personal experiences. Once again using beliefs to change something within the world. Similarly Mahalia Jackson’s signing specifically within the March on Washington for Jobs and Freedom. She was a gospel singer and the daughter of a Baptist minister who would use music to inspire others. Her songs were all deeply rooted in the Christian Faith and echoed the ideas of the Civil Rights Movement.

Religious revivalism also played a huge role and showed that the Civil Rights Movement was a deeply rooted religious one. A lot of people saw the struggle for racial justice as an extension of their faith. Religious leaders also saw the fight for justice as their calling from God.

Religion was a cornerstone of the Civil Rights Movement allowing for a moral foundation and organizing for activism. With the teachings of love, justice, and equality, religious leaders were able to inspire and mobilize the fight for racial justice. The Black Church provided a crucial space for spiritual as well as social action. Allowing for the resilience needed to overcome the challenges faced by African Americans in the pursuit of equality. The Civil Rights Movement serves as a reminder of the power religion holds when used with faith-driven activism in the fight for social justice.
